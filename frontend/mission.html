Mission brief

The AI boom is a
thermal & water crisis.

Hyperscale facilities move heat with evaporative cooling—fast, cheap, and thirsty. Default control waits for danger, then floods entire zones. AquaCool AI reframes cooling as orbital precision: sense the anomaly early, commit water only where the spike will form, and stand down the instant equilibrium returns.

Technologies

Aquifer-class telemetry, ensemble inference, and closed-loop actuation

A full-stack loop unifies sub-second thermal sampling across 24 server zones in four racks, gradient-boosted / random-forest classifiers trained on lag, rolling, spatial neighbor, and calendar features, and proactive liquid cooling that arms before the 85°F trip line—typically around 75–80°F—cutting water per intervention on the order of 60% versus reactive flooding in our simulation stack.

AquaCool mission overview

Three synchronized phases replace “detect and drench” with “predict and pin”—the same reliability class, radically lower hydraulic duty cycle.

Observe

Continuous acquisition across every node: temperature, cooling state, and rack topology with modeled heat diffusion to adjacent servers. Readings stream into a rolling buffer for training and live inference at a one-second cadence.

24 zones · 4 racks · 1 Hz mesh

Infer

Ensemble machine learning scores spike probability from 37+ engineered signals—temporal cycles, multi-step lags, rolling means and variance, neighbor aggregates, and short-horizon acceleration—so the controller authorizes pre-cooling before hard thresholds are violated.

>70% probability → pre-arm

Interdict

Liquid cooling engages in a narrow thermal band, using partial flow instead of maximum deluge. A reactive failsafe remains at 85°F, but the objective is to never arrive there in AI mode: spikes are absorbed in the high-70s/low-80s with materially less water mass per event.

~0.8 gal vs ~2.0 gal reactive

Why this problem matters now

  • Data-center cooling already draws on the order of hundreds of billions of gallons of fresh water annually worldwide—and AI training clusters are accelerating load growth.
  • Large campuses can evaporate millions of gallons per day through towers; every percentage point of hydraulic efficiency is watershed-scale at fleet scope.
  • Reactive control optimizes for binary safety, not resource stewardship: it ignores the shape of the approaching spike until the plant is forced into its highest-flow regime.
